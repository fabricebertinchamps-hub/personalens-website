Why does my partner shut down during arguments?

If your partner consistently goes quiet, gets short, or physically leaves during arguments, your instinct is probably to read it as punishment or rejection. The research suggests something different: most adult shutdown during conflict is a physiological event, not a tactical one. Here's the science.

What's actually happening in their body

John Gottman's lab measured the physiology of couples in conflict for decades. They found that when one partner's heart rate crosses approximately 100 beats per minute during an argument, their nervous system enters a state called 'diffuse physiological arousal' — DPA.

In DPA, the body shifts into threat response. Blood is redirected to the muscles for fight-or-flight. The prefrontal cortex (where complex reasoning happens) is partially shut down. Hearing comprehension narrows. The ability to access language for nuanced topics declines sharply.

This is not the partner choosing to shut down. It's their body unable to do the cognitive work the conversation requires.

Who this happens to more

Gottman's research found men go into DPA at significantly lower thresholds than women in heterosexual couples, on average. The exact mechanism is debated — possibly evolutionary, possibly cultural — but the pattern is consistent across studies.

It also disproportionately affects: people with avoidant attachment, people with childhood trauma involving conflict, people on the autism spectrum, and people with sensory processing differences. None of these mean the person is being manipulative.

What it looks like in texts (because the shutdown carries over)

  • Long reply suddenly becomes one-word reply
  • Slows from minutes to hours of response time, then stops
  • Returns hours later with a topic change as if nothing happened
  • Refuses to engage with emotional content — only logistics
  • Sometimes goes for walks, sleeps, or 'needs to take a shower' mid-conversation

What helps (specifically)

If you're the partner who shuts down

Build a 'time-out' bridge phrase. Something like: 'My system is flooded. I need 30 minutes. I'm not leaving. I'll come back.' Then actually come back. Without this bridge, your partner reads silence as withdrawal of love. With it, they can wait. The Gottmans found that 20–30 minutes of physiological calm-down is what's needed before re-engaging.

If you're the partner of someone who shuts down

Try to recognize the threshold before it crosses. If you can tell their body is escalating, suggest the time-out before they hit DPA. After the time-out, return to the conversation deliberately — don't pretend it didn't happen.

Don't chase during the shutdown. Sending more messages, demanding engagement, raising the volume — all of these prolong the physiological state. The partner literally cannot process what you're sending. Wait.

When shutdown isn't physiology

Distinguishing physiological shutdown from punishment matters. Physiological shutdown is consistent (happens at similar thresholds), brief once removed from the conflict, and the partner comes back wanting to repair. Punishment shutdown is selective (used around topics they want to control), prolonged regardless of physiology, and the partner comes back only when you concede.

If the pattern matches punishment more than physiology, this is closer to coercive control. Different problem, different solution. See our guide on the silent treatment.

Frequently asked questions

Is shutdown the same as stonewalling?

Gottman uses 'stonewalling' for the broader pattern. Physiological shutdown is one cause of stonewalling. Deliberate punishment is another. The behaviors look similar; the underlying mechanisms are different.

How long does the time-out need to be?

Gottman's research suggests 20–30 minutes minimum for the nervous system to return to baseline. Less than that and you re-enter the conflict still flooded. Some people need longer.

My partner says they need 'space' for days. Is that physiological?

Days is past physiology. Hours is physiology. Days suggests either avoidant regulation, punishment, or both. The conversation about the time-out length is its own important conversation.
